X-BAR

CONCEPTION TO MARKET

To create a towbar range for 4x4 vehicles designed specifically for the off-roading market

12+ month project at AutoPacific Group

BACKGROUND

COMPANY

Whilst working at AutoPacific group in Melbourne, I had the opportunity to work as lead project engineer on their flagship off-roading towbar range.

  • Tier 1 automotive supplier holding OEM towing contract for all major automotive companies
  • 80% market share of aftermarket towing
  • Best-in-class and industry standard
  • 65+ years of towing experience
  • Complete vertical integration of supply chain

CONTEXT

The X-BAR was developed under our flagship Hayman Reese brand in response to competitors' products aimed specifically towards the off-roading market.

The size and popularity of the Australian off-roading market is far greater than here in the UK, with SUVs being the largest segment in the car market, accounting for 45.5% of total sales and 3 of the 5 top selling cars in 2019.

When off-roading in deep-water, mud or sand, even the best equipped vehicles can get stuck. The ability to recover a vehicle is essential. Although towballs are occasionally used for this purpose, they are not designed or tested to this capacity and can result in catastrophic failure. Specifically designed recovery hooks should be incorporated into the design.
Off-roading terrain is trecherous for vehicle components; build up of sand, mud & dust leads to a reduction in performance and eventually failure. Rocks and other debris thrown up by the wheels are also a major concern and can easily damage fragile components such as trailer wiring. A means to guard against this would be necessary.
Vehicles designed specifically to go off-road will often have increased ground clearance. One of the most important aspects of this is the approach and departure angles for going over uneven and steep terrain. Towbars can often be one of the lowest points on a vehicle and significantly affect it's departure angle. In order to reduce the towbar's impact it should be as high as possible and follow the contours of the vehicle.
Serious off-roading, heavier towing and a high recovery rating, would require a stronger towbar, designed to withstand greater loads than normal.

DEVELOPMENT

Analysis was conducted on the initial design using Finite Element Analysis modelling in ANSYS. This helped to give a reasonable estimate of how the design would respond in the field. Adjustments were then made to the design where necessary to alleviate high stress areas.

As with all products, the initial design concept had to pass a 'Gate' or peer review stage. The design was critiqued and analysed by those involved with all elements of the supply chain. Not only was this process integral to eliminating errors in the design, it was also instrumental in encouraging me to think holistically about my designs, with the whole supply chain in mind from purchasing and manufacturing to warehouseing and sales.

After the design was accepted through peer review, a prototype was fabricated for testing in our NATA accredited laboratory.

The prototype towbar had to be capable of passing a number of tests in order to be compliant with the relevant vehicle standard: ADR 62. Testing was comprised of: static tension and compression overloading of 1.5 x Aggregate Trailer Mass, cyclical loading of +/- 2.52kN for 2 million cycles and chain-connection max loading.

The recovery points also needed to be testing to ensure they were capable of withstanding the specified forces. These were sent to an external lab for testing.

The final prototype bars were then fitted to the vehicles they were designed for. This highlighted any unforseen issues with the compatability of the design and allowed for marketing photos to be taken and detailed step-by-step fitting instructions to be drawn up.

Once the design was more or less finalised, various parts needed to be sourced. As many elements of the design were new for the company, this involved experimenting with the in-house manufacturing capabilities and outsourcing where appropriate.

All new outsourced parts had to be tested to ensure they would perform in the field: fasteners (testing to breaking + slip testing), recovery hooks (testing to failure - done externally), springs (salt-spray test chamber) and bushes (salt spray test chamber).

FINAL DESIGN

High-tensile grade 10.9 fasteners, 700 grade steel body and forged towball mount.

ENGINEERED FOR STRENGTH

A standardised centre-section across the majority of designs allowed for streamlining and more economical tooling.

STANDARDISED DESIGN

Spring loaded trailer plug housing provided protection for electrical components.

PLUG HOUSING

1 x 8,000kg and 2 x 4,000kg rated forged recovery points provide ability to get nearly any 4x4 out of trouble.
