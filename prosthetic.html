IMPLANT TEST MACHINE

CONCEPT DESIGN

Design a bespoke machine for testing the strength and durability of hip implant prosthetics

3rd Year University Design Project

BACKGROUND

In the UK alone, roughly 100,000 hip replacement procedures are performed every year. A significant proportion of these procedures are to replace previous implants which have fractured. With an ageing population, the total number of procedures continues to rise annually, putting pressure upon the NHS - carrying out the majority of procedures at no cost to the patient.

It is therefore essential to ensure that implants are able to withstand the forces they will be subject to during their lifecycle without failure. This project aims to design a bespoke machine which is able to test a range of prosthetic hip implants.

In order to efficiently test specimens, the machine must simulate and apply the forces experienced during walking over a range of 10,000,000 steps (cycles) or to failure.

DEVELOPMENT

  • Research

    Published data was used to examine the orthogonal forces hip joints are subject to during walking. In order to replicate the resultant force experienced when walking, the machine must apply this distribution of force along each component. Research into the materials used in prosthetics was also undertaken to understand the potential strength requirements of the machine.

  • Environment

    The machine was designed to be installed in a testing laboratory. This widened the potential design options due to availability of water. As the test machine may be pushing the prosthetics to fracture, safety elements would need to be considered in case anything went wrong. Both human activated and automatic emergency stops are standard with most modern laboratory machinery, as well as necessary guards and protective barriers.

  • Ideation

    Due to the practical nature of the machine, its functionality was the main consideration for its design. As a highly technical solution was required, a large component of design development involved research into practical methods of applying the necessary force. Various motorized methods were examined as well as looking at pneumatic and hydraulic possibilities. The range in shape and size of prosthetic also posed a problem as it meant a fixed point at each end of the prosthetic would not work.

  • Functionality

    Research and initial design concepts explored hydraulic and pneumatic loading systems but electromechanical actuators were eventually chosen due to their relatively low complexity, low maintenance, higher degree of control over load profile and the ability to significantly reduce cycle time whilst still accurately applying the necessary loads.

CALCULATING

It was important to ensure that the design of the machine could withstand the forces it was applying throughout its life cycle. Due to the cyclic nature of the forces, the main potential failure mechanism was fatigue. The maximum potential stress and maximum shaft deflection of critical elements was calculated and applied with a safety factor to give the lower limits for dimensions and material strength necessary. Further calculations were necessary in choosing bearings, lead screws and actuators.

    Top end of the prosthetic is held in place by a circular chuck. The chuck is mounted on a worktable which is able to be moved horizontally along both axes. This accommodates for different sized prosthetics.
